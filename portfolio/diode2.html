Additive Manufacturing of a Microfluidic Diode

A collection of work over the course of my career and education.

Introduction

Here we examine the design process and performance of six valve designs printed with multi-jet modeling technology. The ultimate goal of the design challenge is to create a microfluidic diode that experimentally shows the largest Diodicity - the ratio of ’allowed’ to ’obstructed’ fluid flow. Here, we investigated the design, manufacturing, and testing of microfluidic diodes to increase diodicity - the ratio of ’allowed’ to ’obstructed’ fluid flow through the diode. The multi-jet model- ing (MJM) printer used in this experiment, the Stratasys Objet500 Connex3, allowed the simultaneous printing of both flexible Tan- goBlack FLX973 and rigid VeroClear RGD810 materials. Problem

Develop a levitation system that can support the weight of the pod, allow high speed travel and minimize the response to vibration.

System Description

Passive magnetic levitation reduces friction with the track and avoiding common issues with wheels at high speeds. While magnetic drag has a large peak at low velocities prior to levitation, its lift to drag ratio only improves with speed.

Design

  • The levitation array was designed to be able to levitate the pod after being pushed up to 5-6m/s.
  • The suspension was designed hold the weight of the frame, and to absorb any the loads during an acceleration or brake of 1g.
  • Each levitation array required a minimum harmonic frequency of 120 Hz, to safely avoid the frequency of gaps in the track at speed and to allow for safer handling.
